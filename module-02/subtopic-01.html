Rivest-Shamir-Adleman (RSA) Algorithm

The RSA algorithm is named after Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman, who originally published the algorithm in 1977. The RSA algorithm is an asymmetric, or public-key cryptography algorithm, which means that it works using two different keys: a public key and a private key. The public key is used for encryption and is known by everyone, while the private key is used for decryption, and is kept secret by the receiver.

How It Works

The RSA algorithm is based on the factorization of large numbers and modular arithmetic for encrypting and decrypting data. It consists of 3 main stages:

Key Generation

Two large prime numbers p and q are chosen and multiplied to produce n = p × q, called the modulus. From this, Euler's totient φ(n) = (p−1)(q−1) is computed. A public exponent e is chosen (commonly 65537), and a private exponent d is derived such that e × d ≡ 1 (mod φ(n)). The public key is the pair (e, n); the private key is (d, n).

Encryption

To send a confidential message M to someone, the sender obtains the recipient's public key (e, n) and computes the ciphertext: C = Me mod n. This ciphertext can be transmitted over any public channel. Without the private key, an attacker cannot reverse the operation.

Decryption

Only the recipient, holding the private key (d, n), can recover the original message by computing: M = Cd mod n. The mathematical relationship between e and d guarantees that this reverses the encryption exactly. No one else can perform this step without knowing d.

How It's Used

RSA is used for digital signatures and for encrypting small pieces of data — most commonly the symmetric session key used in TLS/HTTPS connections. Because RSA is computationally expensive for large data, modern protocols use RSA to securely exchange a symmetric key, then switch to faster symmetric encryption (like AES) for the bulk of the data transfer.

Key sizes matter: 512-bit RSA was broken in 1999, 768-bit in 2009. NIST currently recommends a minimum of 2048 bits, with 3072 or 4096 bits for long-term security needs.
